Accessing the Risk of Wildlife-Vehicle Collisions for Black Bears (Ursus americanus) in Southern Florida: A Land-Use Perspective

Spatial Database Development in Practice-2023 Spring
Final Project
Naoya Morishita , Yu-Yun Ruan

Introduction

This study delves into the impact of roads on vulnerable animal populations in Florida, emphasizing the risk of extinction due to wildlife-vehicle conflict and roadkill. It examines black bear roadkill records, land use changes, and environmental predictors, aiming to inform infrastructure management and protective measures for animal populations.

Utilized PostgreSQL with spatial functions like ST_Buffer() and ST_Intersection to assess land changes around bear mortality, focusing on collisions in the south post-2000. Buffers with different radii were applied based on gender-specific home range estimates. For the prediction model, we employed linear regression, creating a grid using ST_Creategrid and clipping it to match land use data. ST_Contains selected grids with bear mortality, ST_Area calculated urban areas, and linear regression explored the relationship between land use changes and black bear WVC.

Result

This study analyzed 5260 black bear road mortality records from 1976 to 2023. Most collisions occurred in the Northeast and Northwest regions, with 400 in the southern region, mainly in Collier County. Temporally, a peak occurred in 2021-2022, with male bears, particularly young ones, more prone to road collisions. Land use changes from 1995 to 2014-2016 revealed increased urbanization, deforestation, and water areas, particularly near mortality sites. Deforestation and barren land loss were more pronounced near mortality locations than statewide trends.

In the data preparation of the prediction model part, 62 grids (10km side length) containing 275 black bear records were selected. Urban area increased from 558.5 to 886.8 square kilometers between 1995 and 2015. However, linear regression (y = -0.056x + 4.49, R2 = 0.0007) showed no significant correlation between urban area change and black bear roadkill, rendering the prediction model invalid.
