Analysis of the Relationship Between Vegetation Productivity and Pollution in Massachusetts

Advanced Raster GIS -2023 Fall
Final Project
Abby Beilman, Naoya Morishita, Adlai Nelson, Yu-Yun Ruan

Introduction

Climate change, driven by human activities, impacts global vegetation health and air quality. While existing research explores various factors influencing vegetation productivity and air quality, this study focuses on their intricate relationship in Massachusetts. It addresses research gaps and connects findings to broader climate studies in the region, aiming to compare trends in vegetation (Normalized difference vegetation index, NDVI) and air quality (Aerosol, AOD) over time.

This project processed MODIS data in Google Earth Engine. The QA-masked data were analyzed using the Mann-Kendall Tau test to identify trends without assuming data distribution. Theil-Sen Slope Analysis generated median slopes for NDVI and AOD. This research selected 10 sample point data to compare pixel-level mean values.

Conclusion

NDVI trends in Massachusetts show spatial variability, with predominantly negative trends, except for positive trends in the Southeast. However, pixel-level analysis reveals increasing NDVI in the Southeast. Air quality has improved statewide over 20 years, but greenness decreased, except in the Southeast. AOD values spiked in 2002 and 2010, while NDVI remained stable. Linear regression and Theil-Sen slope analyses confirmed randomness and a lack of significant trends. Future research will explore industrial dynamics, climate change impacts, and finer temporal resolutions. This study highlights the need for comprehensive analysis to understand the complex interplay between air quality and vegetation dynamics, offering insights into environmental trends.
